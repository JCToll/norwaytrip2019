Our Norway experience had a variety of many different adventures. We saw such diverse regions with different landscapes and experiences.
This web page is divided out into the 6 regions and cities that we visited, in the order that we visted as shown in the map below:

1. Oslo – the capital city, known for its green spaces and museums. We spent 3 days (July 9-11).

2. Sognefjord – nicknamed the King of the Fjords, is the largest and deepest fjord in Norway; 2 days (July 12-13).

3. Bergen -- known as the 'city of seven mountains' and was a Hanseatic trading post during the 13th to 18th centuries ; 3 days (July 14-16).

4. Solstrand – a resort and spa just South of Bergen on the Bjørnafjorden fjord; 2 days.(July 17-18).

5. Andøya Island – 200 miles N. of the Arctic Circle, where my grandparents grew up; 2 days (July 19-20)

6. Lofoten Islands – an archipelago of 6+ islands known for dramatic scenery of mountains and arctic fjords; 3 days (July 21-23)

After our experience, what advice would I give to others wanting to travel to Norway?

First, visiting both Oslo and Bergen, is a must if it’s your first visit. I wished we had one more day in Oslo, because there is plenty to do and see in the capital.

After visiting the “big cities”, plan at least one adventure to a smaller village, a fjord, or a more remote area. We chose to spend a day and night at Balestrand on a fjord; I just wished we had spent more time there.

If you want to explore a more remote area, I highly recommend the Lofoten Islands – they were magical, and the next time we visit Norway, I would like to spend 4 to 5 days exploring this area. Just don’t do what we did – we started and ended in Harstad, which was just too far away, and also was a rather boring city. Instead, fly directly into Svolvaer, which has car rentals; this will save you time. Another option is to fly into Bodø, rent a car and take the ferry across to the Lofoten Islands.

If you have relatives, even distant relatives, make a point to visit them, even if it’s just for a quick coffee. They can give you the local perspective and help you with your plans. We were fortunate to have relatives that my mother is close to, so it was easier to arrange the encounters. The visits with our relatives were really the highlight of the trip.

And now, advice about the challenges of visiting Norway:

First, you need to plan far ahead, for everything. Summer is a very busy tourist season, and hotels, AirBnb’s, trains, planes, boats – are quickly sold out. And be prepared to be inundated by tourists. We seldom experienced the crowd of tourists, partly because we went early to museums, and managed to adjust our tourist times. Bergen can get several (up to 10!) cruise ships docked at once, so it can be crowded, but mostly in the downtown Brygenn wharf area and the funicular.

Costs issues – yes Norway can be expensive. We found hotels to be reasonably priced – and they all offer free big breakfasts. Eating out at restaurants is expensive. However, you have to account for the fact that the taxes are included in the pricing, and there is really no tipping done, except a small amount at nicer restaurants. So when considering restaurant prices, take off 20% or more to match US costs. Drinks, especially alcohol, are very expensive. If you minimize this, you can save on costs.

Should you use cash or credit cards? Not once did we use cash, or use an ATM to extract cash. Everyplace in Norway uses credit cards, even the street merchants. We were advised at our first hotel, to just use credit cards, and it worked for us, even in the more remote areas.

Renting a car in Norway can be very expensive. We needed a car to explore Andøya and the Lofoten Islands, so we had little choice. You have to "pay to play".
